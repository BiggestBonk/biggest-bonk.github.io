Neuroplasticity

How does understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefit people?
Understanding neuroplasticity and its nuances can benefit people in a variety of ways, most of which revolve around how they frame their learning experiences. For example, if someone's learning style has been fairly rigid since they left highschool, they might expect all learning to be a dull affair in which you get bombarded by information and then get assessed on how well you retained it. The fundamentals of neuroplasticity push back against that idea, instead showing that the mind becomes more engaged and malleable when it’s given things that engage and challenge its expectations.
Also, understanding specific principles like “use it or lose it” can be instrumental in realizing that practice really does make perfect, and that leaving a specific skill behind or falling out of routine can have a very real, tangible impact on how well you can retain said abilities.
How might you engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit?
Engaging with these principles recently has been helping me throughout day to day life, especially in an educational context. The first of these principles I’ve been utilizing is “use it and improve it”. Before starting at Dev Academy, I began working through the Odin project for at least a few hours each day, so that I could come in with a sense of structure and discipline that was otherwise lacking, whilst also improving my technical skills and learning along the way. Another one of these principles I’ve been trying to practice regularly is “repetition matters”. Whenever I come back from the weekend to dive in once again, I tend to find myself feeling a bit confused and overwhelmed once I hit the point where I need to recall previously learned information. When that happens though, I’ve found it super useful to start looking through useful resources I’ve bookmarked and giving them another readthrough, as it reinforces that previously only partially retained information. I may not fully retain the information fully if it comes back around again, but there is a gradual improvement.
What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?
There are plenty of ways to improve neuroplasticity throughout your day to day life, with a fair few of them even seeming small and inconsequential, but they’re far from it. The first of these is exercise. Exercise can sometimes seem antithetical (or opposite) to learning, given that you don’t have your head stuck into a book or hands hammering away at a keyboard, but it can be for that exact reason that it can be so beneficial. Generally, when you’re exercising you’ll be getting your body moving and a lot of its systems firing, which empowers the development of nerve cells and increases the connections between your brain’s synapses. Another way to increase
your neuroplasticity is oddly enough, changing your vocabulary and the way you mentally phrase things. If you’re one that's quick to throw around phrases like “I hate this” or “I’m failing, kill me now” genuinely just trying to be more fair to the task at hand, or if you’re anything like me just easing up on yourself. Replacing the “kill me now” at the end of your sentences with “I just don’t have it quite yet” can be shockingly impactful, as you’ve taken some of that dastardly self blame out and replaced it with something a lot more reasonable. At least from my experience, this can really help with not getting caught up in a thought spiral that would’ve otherwise just eaten up time and made me feel worse.

The Growth Mindset

What is the “Growth Mindset” and why is it relevant?
The growth mindset is an approach to facing challenges or unforeseen setbacks that revolves around understanding that the struggle is part of the process, rather than a personal failure. It is incredibly relevant in the field of web and software development more broadly as hitting stumbling blocks is nigh on unavoidable in this field. If you were to approach programming with the alternate “fixed mindset” you wouldn’t last long before having your entire sense of self detonate when you inevitably run into a bug. The more I keep learning to program, the more obvious it becomes that perseverance is the name of the game, and the growth mindset is instrumental in building it.
In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?
I can’t say anything in particular has changed in this instance, but when I first heard about it, it flipped my established learning style on its head. When I began The Odin Project, I was still very much in the traditional highschool mindset thinking that failure of any variety was catastrophic and a negative mark on me as a person. I used to find myself burning out a lot and just giving up if something felt as if it wasn’t clicking the first time around. My previous mindset was very restrictive in the way that I thought I couldn’t take the time to gain skills when they were absent in the first place, whereas now I understand that being inexperienced in something generally means that you won’t be good at it immediately, and only delving into things you already have some talent for makes the scope of what you can learn significantly restricted.
How will you integrate a growth mindset into your learning journey?
I feel like my previous answers already address this question well enough, but I’ll continue raving for a few more sentences. More than anything I feel that I need to approach this course and any greater context with some sense of compassion, so that I can continue to work through things I find challenging or even at times insurmountable without spiraling. I’ve already had quite a few moments here and during the Odin Project where I’ve really felt like I was in a hole and ready to give up, but every time so far and (hopefully) every time in future, I hope I can push through dust myself and persist regardless of the challenges I face.
