Margin, Border and Paddng in CSS: What's The Difference?

Starting from the top, If you're just getting familiarised with CSS, you've probably heard about margin, padding or even border in passing to some degree but might still have no idea what they mean, let alone how they'd help with web design. All of these concepts/rules perform similar tasks when it comes to styling your webpage in CSS, but it is crucial to understand each one's unique attributes to get the best use out of the tools at your disposal.

Padding

Put simply, padding is the space between your content (text in a paragraph, for example) and the border (outer edge) of your element. Padding is useful for creating extra space inside your element, so it doesn't clash against other nearby elements or aspects of the page. Padding is at it's best (or in it's element, if you will) when you want to position your text boxes and images seperately, without throwing off their alignment.

Margin

Margin, on the other hand, is the space around your element. It's value defines how much space the given element should have between the other nearby elements. Margin is great for for giving elements space that feels purposeful and can reduce the claustrophobic, cramped feeling websites can sometimes fall into.

Border

Lastly, border is the perimeter of the box/element. It's certainly the lesser of these three in terms of practicality, but it can still come in handy for adding some visual flair or giving an element some definition. Border can be styled in a variety of ways including (but not limited to) solid line, dotted line, or even entirely invisible.
